Q: Which planet is closest to the Sun?
A: Mercury.

Q: How long is a year on Mercury?
A: A year on Mercury is about 88 Earth days.

Q: Does Mercury have an atmosphere?
A: Mercury has a very thin, tenuous atmosphere called an exosphere, composed mostly of oxygen, sodium, hydrogen, helium, and potassium.

Q: What are the temperature extremes on Mercury?
A: Mercury's surface temperature can reach up to 800°F (430°C) during the day and drop to -290°F (-180°C) at night.

Q: Does Mercury have any moons?
A: No, Mercury has no moons.

Q: What is the surface of Mercury like?
A: Mercury's surface is heavily cratered, similar to Earth's Moon, indicating a long history of impacts.

Q: What is the Caloris Basin?
A: The Caloris Basin is the largest impact crater on Mercury, about 1,550 kilometers in diameter.

Q: How long is a day on Mercury?
A: A solar day on Mercury (one full day-night cycle) is about 176 Earth days long, which is longer than its year.

Q: What is Mercury's core made of?
A: Mercury has a large, metallic core, primarily made of iron, which makes up about 85% of the planet's radius.

Q: Has Mercury been visited by spacecraft?
A: Yes, by NASA's Mariner 10 and MESSENGER missions, and the ongoing BepiColombo mission (ESA/JAXA).

Q: Why does Mercury have such extreme temperature swings?
A: Its lack of a substantial atmosphere to trap heat causes extreme temperature differences between day and night.

Q: What are "hollows" on Mercury?
A: Hollows are unique, irregular, and bright depressions on Mercury's surface that appear to be actively forming.

Q: Is there ice on Mercury?
A: Yes, water ice exists in permanently shadowed craters near Mercury's poles where temperatures are consistently very low.

Q: What is Mercury's orbital eccentricity?
A: Mercury has the most eccentric (least circular) orbit of all the planets in the solar system.

Q: What is a "spin-orbit resonance"?
A: It's a relationship where a body's rotation period and orbital period are a ratio of two small integers. Mercury has a 3:2 spin-orbit resonance, rotating three times for every two orbits around the Sun.

Q: How was Mercury named?
A: It was named after the Roman messenger god, known for his speed, because of the planet's swift orbit around the Sun.

Q: What is the dominant geological process on Mercury?
A: Impact cratering is the dominant process, but evidence of volcanism and tectonic activity (scarps) is also present.

Q: Is Mercury tidally locked to the Sun?
A: No, it is not tidally locked in a 1:1 ratio like the Moon is to Earth. It has a 3:2 spin-orbit resonance.

Q: Which planet is known as Earth's "sister planet"?
A: Venus, due to its similar size, mass, and composition.

Q: Why is Venus the hottest planet in the solar system?
A: Its thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide creates a powerful runaway greenhouse effect, trapping heat and raising surface temperatures to over 880°F (470°C).

Q: What is the atmosphere of Venus primarily composed of?
A: The atmosphere is about 96.5% carbon dioxide, with small amounts of nitrogen and other trace gases.

Q: How long is a day on Venus?
A: A day on Venus (243 Earth days) is longer than its year (225 Earth days).

Q: In which direction does Venus rotate?
A: Venus rotates retrograde, or backwards, compared to most other planets in the solar system. From the surface, the Sun would appear to rise in the west and set in the east.

Q: Does Venus have any moons?
A: No, Venus has no moons.

Q: What are the clouds of Venus made of?
A: The clouds are composed of droplets of sulfuric acid.

Q: What is the surface pressure on Venus?
A: The surface pressure on Venus is over 90 times that of Earth, equivalent to the pressure found about 1 kilometer deep in Earth's oceans.

Q: Has any spacecraft successfully landed on Venus?
A: Yes, the Soviet Union's Venera program successfully landed several probes on the surface, which transmitted data for a short time before succumbing to the harsh conditions.

Q: Does Venus have volcanoes?
A: Yes, Venus is covered with thousands of volcanoes, and some may still be active. Maat Mons is its largest shield volcano.

Q: What are "pancake domes" on Venus?
A: Pancake domes are unusual, flat-topped volcanic features formed by the eruption of thick, viscous lava.

Q: Why is Venus so bright in the sky?
A: Its thick, highly reflective clouds of sulfuric acid reflect about 70% of the sunlight that hits them, making it the brightest planet as seen from Earth.

Q: Does Venus have a magnetic field?
A: No, Venus has an extremely weak magnetic field, likely due to its very slow rotation.

Q: How was Venus named?
A: It was named after the Roman goddess of love and beauty.

Q: What are coronae on Venus?
A: Coronae are large, circular, crown-like structures on Venus's surface, thought to be formed by upwellings of hot material from the mantle.

Q: Can we see the surface of Venus with a standard telescope?
A: No, the surface is permanently obscured by its thick cloud cover. We use radar mapping from orbiting spacecraft to see the surface.

Q: What is a transit of Venus?
A: A transit of Venus is when the planet passes directly between the Sun and Earth, appearing as a small black dot crossing the Sun's face.

Q: Is there any evidence of water on Venus?
A: While Venus is incredibly dry now, scientists believe it may have had large amounts of water in its distant past, which were lost to space.

Q: What is the only planet known to support life?
A: Earth.

Q: What is the composition of Earth's atmosphere?
A: Earth's atmosphere is approximately 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, 0.9% argon, and 0.1% other gases, including carbon dioxide.

Q: What is the name of Earth's natural satellite?
A: The Moon.

Q: What causes the seasons on Earth?
A: The tilt of Earth's rotational axis (about 23.5 degrees) relative to its orbital plane around the Sun.

Q: What is the Earth's magnetic field called?
A: The magnetosphere. It protects the planet from harmful solar winds.

Q: What are the main layers of Earth's interior?
A: The crust, the mantle, the outer core (liquid), and the inner core (solid).

Q: What is plate tectonics?
A: The theory that Earth's outer shell is divided into several plates that glide over the mantle, causing earthquakes, volcanoes, and the creation of mountains.

Q: How old is the Earth?
A: Approximately 4.54 billion years old.

Q: What percentage of Earth's surface is covered by water?
A: About 71 percent.

Q: What is the greenhouse effect on Earth?
A: A natural process where certain gases in the atmosphere trap heat, keeping the planet warm enough for life. Human activities have enhanced this effect.

Q: What is the ozone layer?
A: A region of Earth's stratosphere that absorbs most of the Sun's ultraviolet (UV) radiation.

Q: What is the diameter of the Earth?
A: The equatorial diameter is about 12,756 kilometers (7,926 miles).

Q: What is the "Goldilocks Zone"?
A: The habitable zone around a star where the temperature is just right—not too hot and not too cold—for liquid water to exist on a planet's surface. Earth is in this zone.

Q: What are the Van Allen radiation belts?
A: Zones of energetic charged particles, held in place by Earth's magnetic field.

Q: How long does it take for light from the Sun to reach Earth?
A: About 8 minutes and 20 seconds.

Q: What is Earth's orbital speed?
A: Earth orbits the Sun at an average speed of about 30 kilometers per second (67,000 miles per hour).

Q: What is the Great Oxygenation Event?
A: A period in Earth's history when cyanobacteria produced enough oxygen to fundamentally change the composition of the atmosphere, allowing for the evolution of complex life.

Q: Why is Mars known as the "Red Planet"?
A: Its surface is rich in iron oxide (rust), which gives it a reddish-orange appearance.

Q: Does Mars have moons?
A: Yes, Mars has two small, irregularly shaped moons named Phobos and Deimos.

Q: What is the largest volcano in the solar system?
A: Olympus Mons, a massive shield volcano on Mars, is the largest volcano and tallest mountain in the solar system.

Q: What is the atmosphere of Mars like?
A: Mars has a very thin atmosphere, composed mainly of carbon dioxide (about 95%).

Q: Is there water on Mars?
A: Yes, water exists on Mars primarily as ice in the polar ice caps and buried underground. There is strong evidence for liquid brines flowing seasonally.

Q: What is Valles Marineris?
A: Valles Marineris is a vast canyon system on Mars, one of the largest in the solar system, stretching over 4,000 kilometers long.

Q: How long is a day on Mars?
A: A Martian day, called a "sol," is very similar to an Earth day: about 24.6 hours.

Q: How long is a year on Mars?
A: A year on Mars is about 687 Earth days, roughly twice as long as an Earth year.

Q: What are the polar ice caps on Mars made of?
A: They are composed of a combination of water ice and frozen carbon dioxide (dry ice).

Q: Have rovers explored Mars?
A: Yes, several rovers, including Sojourner, Spirit, Opportunity, Curiosity, and Perseverance, have explored the Martian surface.

Q: Does Mars have seasons?
A: Yes, Mars has seasons similar to Earth because of the tilt of its rotational axis.

Q: What is the temperature on Mars?
A: Temperatures on Mars are very cold, with an average of about -80°F (-60°C).

Q: How was Mars named?
A: It was named after the Roman god of war, due to its reddish, blood-like color.

Q: Why can't liquid water exist for long on the surface of Mars today?
A: The low atmospheric pressure would cause any liquid water to quickly boil away.

Q: What are "dust devils" on Mars?
A: Dust devils are rotating columns of air and dust, similar to those on Earth but often much larger, that are common on Mars.

Q: What is the primary mission of the Perseverance rover?
A: To search for signs of ancient microbial life and collect rock and soil samples for possible return to Earth.

Q: Does Mars have a global magnetic field?
A: No, Mars does not have a global magnetic field, but it does have localized areas of strong crustal magnetism.

Q: What is a Martian "blueberry"?
A: Small, spherical, iron-rich concretions discovered by the Opportunity rover, suggesting the past presence of water.

Q: What is the largest planet in our solar system?
A: Jupiter.

Q: What is Jupiter primarily made of?
A: Jupiter is a gas giant, composed mainly of hydrogen and helium.

Q: What is the Great Red Spot?
A: The Great Red Spot is a gigantic, long-lived storm on Jupiter, wider than the Earth.

Q: How many moons does Jupiter have?
A: Jupiter has over 90 confirmed moons, the most of any planet in the solar system.

Q: What are the Galilean moons?
A: They are Jupiter's four largest moons, discovered by Galileo Galilei: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.

Q: Which of Jupiter's moons is the most volcanically active body in the solar system?
A: Io.

Q: Which moon is thought to have a vast liquid water ocean under its icy shell?
A: Europa.

Q: What is the largest moon in the solar system?
A: Ganymede, one of Jupiter's moons, is larger than the planet Mercury.

Q: Does Jupiter have rings?
A: Yes, Jupiter has a faint ring system composed mainly of dust particles.

Q: How long is a day on Jupiter?
A: Jupiter has the shortest day of any planet, rotating once in just under 10 hours.

Q: How long is a year on Jupiter?
A: A year on Jupiter is about 12 Earth years.

Q: Does Jupiter have a solid surface?
A: No, as a gas giant, Jupiter does not have a solid surface. The gas just gets denser with depth.

Q: What is Jupiter's magnetic field like?
A: Jupiter has the strongest magnetic field of any planet in the solar system, nearly 20,000 times stronger than Earth's.

Q: What is the Juno mission?
A: A NASA mission currently orbiting Jupiter to study its composition, gravity field, magnetic field, and polar magnetosphere.

Q: How was Jupiter named?
A: It was named after the king of the Roman gods.

Q: What causes the distinct bands and zones in Jupiter's atmosphere?
A: They are caused by strong east-west winds and differences in the chemistry and temperature of the atmospheric gases, creating belts (darker bands) and zones (lighter bands).

Q_ What is metallic hydrogen?
A: A phase of hydrogen that conducts electricity like a metal. It is believed to exist deep within Jupiter and Saturn, generating their strong magnetic fields.

Q: Did a comet ever impact Jupiter?
A: Yes, in 1994, fragments of Comet Shoemaker-Levy 9 collided with Jupiter, leaving visible impact scars in its atmosphere.

Q: Which planet is famous for its extensive ring system?
A: Saturn.

Q: What are Saturn's rings made of?
A: They are made mostly of billions of small particles of water ice, with some rocky material and dust.

Q: What is the composition of Saturn?
A: Like Jupiter, Saturn is a gas giant composed primarily of hydrogen and helium.

Q: Which planet is the least dense in the solar system?
A: Saturn. It is less dense than water and would float in a large enough bathtub.

Q: What is Saturn's largest moon?
A: Titan.

Q: What is unique about Saturn's moon Titan?
A: Titan is the only moon in the solar system with a thick, dense atmosphere and the only celestial body other than Earth with clear evidence of stable bodies of surface liquid (liquid methane and ethane).

Q: What is unique about Saturn's moon Enceladus?
A: Enceladus has geysers that erupt plumes of water ice and vapor from its subsurface ocean into space.

Q: How long is a day on Saturn?
A: A day on Saturn is about 10.7 hours.

Q: How long is a year on Saturn?
A: A year on Saturn is about 29.5 Earth years.

Q: What is the "Great White Spot"?
A: A massive, periodic storm in Saturn's northern hemisphere that occurs roughly once every 30 years.

Q: What is the hexagonal storm at Saturn's north pole?
A: It is a persistent, six-sided jet stream pattern of clouds at Saturn's north pole.

Q: What spacecraft extensively studied Saturn and its moons?
A: The Cassini-Huygens mission, which orbited Saturn from 2004 to 2017.

Q: How was Saturn named?
A: It was named after the Roman god of agriculture and wealth.

Q: How many main ring groups does Saturn have?
A: Saturn has seven main rings, which are lettered in the order they were discovered (A, B, C, D, E, F, and G).

Q: What are "shepherd moons"?
A: Small moons that orbit near the edges of planetary rings, using their gravity to keep the ring particles in a narrow band. Saturn's F ring is a prime example.

Q: How fast are the winds on Saturn?
A: Saturn has some of the fastest winds in the solar system, reaching speeds of up to 1,800 kilometers per hour (1,100 mph) at the equator.

Q: What are "ring spokes" on Saturn?
A: Transient, radial features in Saturn's B ring that rotate with the planet's magnetic field, thought to be caused by electrostatically levitated dust particles.

Q: What is Iapetus, and what is its most prominent feature?
A: Iapetus is one of Saturn's moons, known for its dramatic two-tone coloration (one side is bright, the other is dark) and a massive equatorial ridge.

Q: Which planet rotates on its side?
A: Uranus has an axial tilt of about 98 degrees, meaning it essentially orbits the Sun on its side.

Q: What gives Uranus its blue-green color?
A: Methane gas in its upper atmosphere absorbs red light and reflects blue and green light.

Q: How was Uranus discovered?
A: It was discovered by William Herschel in 1781, the first planet found with the aid of a telescope.

Q: How was Uranus named?
A: It is named after the Greek god of the sky, Ouranos. It's the only planet named after a Greek deity rather than a Roman one.

Q: What is the internal composition of Uranus?
A: Uranus is an "ice giant," with a small rocky core, a massive icy mantle of water, ammonia, and methane, and an outer atmosphere of hydrogen and helium.

Q: Does Uranus have rings?
A: Yes, Uranus has a system of 13 known, narrow, dark rings.

Q: How many moons does Uranus have?
A: Uranus has 27 known moons, named after characters from the works of William Shakespeare and Alexander Pope.

Q: What are the five largest moons of Uranus?
A: Miranda, Ariel, Umbriel, Titania, and Oberon.

Q: Which spacecraft has visited Uranus?
A: Only one spacecraft, Voyager 2, has flown by Uranus, doing so in 1986.

Q: How long is a year on Uranus?
A: A year on Uranus is about 84 Earth years.

Q: What are seasons like on Uranus?
A: Due to its extreme tilt, seasons are extreme. Each pole gets about 42 years of continuous sunlight followed by 42 years of darkness.

Q: Is Uranus colder than Neptune?
A: Although Neptune is farther from the Sun, Uranus is the coldest planet in the solar system, with minimum temperatures of -224°C (-371°F).

Q: What direction does Uranus rotate?
A: Like Venus, Uranus has a retrograde rotation, spinning clockwise on its axis.

Q: Does Uranus have a magnetic field?
A: Yes, and its magnetic field is unusually tilted at about 60 degrees from its rotational axis and is offset from the planet's center.

Q: What is Miranda known for?
A: Miranda, a moon of Uranus, has one of the most extreme and varied landscapes in the solar system, including giant canyons 20 kilometers deep.

Q: What is the most distant planet from the Sun?
A: Neptune.

Q: How was Neptune discovered?
A: Neptune was the first planet found through mathematical prediction based on observed irregularities in the orbit of Uranus, rather than by direct observation. It was first observed in 1846.

Q: What gives Neptune its deep blue color?
A: Similar to Uranus, methane in its atmosphere absorbs red light, but an unknown component is thought to contribute to its more vivid blue hue.

Q: What are the weather conditions like on Neptune?
A: Neptune has the most extreme and fastest winds in the solar system, reaching speeds of over 2,000 kilometers per hour (1,200 mph).

Q: What was the Great Dark Spot?
A: A large storm system in Neptune's atmosphere, similar to Jupiter's Great Red Spot, that was observed by Voyager 2 in 1989 but had vanished by 1994.

Q: How many moons does Neptune have?
A: Neptune has 14 known moons.

Q: What is Neptune's largest moon?
A: Triton.

Q: What is unique about Triton's orbit?
A: Triton is the only large moon in the solar system with a retrograde orbit, meaning it orbits in the opposite direction of Neptune's rotation.

Q: What are the cryovolcanoes on Triton?
A: Cryovolcanoes, or ice volcanoes, on Triton erupt plumes of nitrogen gas and dark dust particles, which freeze and fall back to the surface.

Q: Does Neptune have rings?
A: Yes, Neptune has a faint and fragmented ring system, with some sections appearing as arcs.

Q: How long is a year on Neptune?
A: A year on Neptune takes about 165 Earth years.

Q: How was Neptune named?
A: It was named after the Roman god of the sea.

Q: Which spacecraft has visited Neptune?
A: Only one spacecraft, Voyager 2, has flown by Neptune, doing so in 1989.

Q: Why is Neptune warmer than expected?
A: Neptune radiates more than twice the energy it receives from the Sun, suggesting it has a significant internal heat source.

Q: What is the definition of a planet?
A: A celestial body that (a) is in orbit around the Sun, (b) has sufficient mass for its self-gravity to overcome rigid body forces so that it assumes a nearly round shape, and (c) has cleared the neighborhood around its orbit.

Q: What is a dwarf planet?
A: A celestial body that meets the first two criteria for a planet but has not cleared the neighborhood around its orbit and is not a satellite.

Q: Why was Pluto reclassified as a dwarf planet?
A: Because it has not cleared its orbital neighborhood of other objects; it shares its orbital space with other bodies in the Kuiper Belt.

Q: What is the largest dwarf planet?
A: Eris is the most massive known dwarf planet, while Pluto is the largest by volume.

Q: Where is the dwarf planet Ceres located?
A: Ceres is located in the main asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter. It is the largest object in that belt.

Q: What is the Kuiper Belt?
A: A doughnut-shaped region of icy bodies beyond the orbit of Neptune, where many dwarf planets, including Pluto, reside.

Q: What is the Oort Cloud?
A: A theoretical, immense spherical cloud of icy planetesimals believed to surround the Sun at distances ranging from 2,000 to 200,000 AU. It is the source of long-period comets.

Q: What are the two main categories of planets in our solar system?
A: Terrestrial planets (rocky, like Earth and Mars) and Jovian or gas giant planets (large and gas-rich, like Jupiter and Saturn). Ice giants Uranus and Neptune are a sub-category of gas giants.

Q: What is an exoplanet?
A: An exoplanet is a planet that orbits a star outside our solar system.

Q: What is the "protoplanetary disk"?
A: A rotating disk of dense gas and dust surrounding a newly formed star, from which planets are eventually formed through accretion.

Q: What is orbital resonance?
A: A situation where orbiting bodies exert a regular, periodic gravitational influence on each other, often due to their orbital periods being related by a ratio of small integers.

Q: What is unique about the dwarf planet Haumea?
A: Haumea has a distinct, elongated, football-like shape due to its extremely rapid rotation and also possesses a ring system.

Q: What is the dwarf planet Makemake named after?
A: Makemake is named after the creator god of the Rapa Nui people of Easter Island.
